# Product

## Register

product

## Users

Better Calories is for mobile users who want calorie and macro tracking to fit into normal eating habits instead of becoming a data-entry chore. They log recurring meals, gym meals, home-cooked food, supermarket products, and quick snacks in the moment, often with incomplete phrasing such as "my usual breakfast" or "150 grams of chicken and rice."

The primary user context is daily personal tracking: checking how many calories remain, confirming a proposed meal, correcting portions, reusing templates, and reviewing recent history. Spain and EU users matter, so metric units, branded packaged foods, and user custom foods are first-class concerns.

## Product Purpose

Better Calories helps users log meals faster and more reliably through natural language, voice, templates, and trusted nutrition sources. The product exists to reduce friction without reducing trust: the app should make a likely meal proposal, show calories and macros clearly, let the user confirm or correct it, then improve recurring meal memory over time.

Success means users can track daily calories and macros with fewer taps than a traditional food diary while still understanding where nutrition values came from and what will be committed to their history.

## Brand Personality

Fresh, trustworthy, lightweight.

The product voice should feel direct, calm, and practical. It should make calorie tracking feel approachable and modern, but never casual about nutrition accuracy. UI copy should be short, human, and action-oriented: propose, confirm, correct, continue.

## Anti-references

Better Calories should not feel like:

- A cluttered MyFitnessPal-style database browser where the user does all the work.
- A clinical diet or medical compliance app.
- A dark gym-performance dashboard dominated by aggressive metrics.
- A chatbot-first interface that hides structured controls behind conversation.
- A spreadsheet-like macro tracker with dense tables as the default surface.
- A generic AI app that invents nutrition values without source clarity or confirmation.

Avoid decorative AI tropes, overcomplicated dashboards, novelty controls for standard tasks, and any UI pattern that makes committed nutrition data feel uncertain.

## Design Principles

1. Speed without guesswork. Logging should be fast, but proposed calories and macros must remain inspectable before they become history.
2. Trust before automation. Confirmation, correction, source clarity, and auditability are product features, not friction.
3. Recurring meals should become effortless. Templates, memory, and "usual meal" flows should reduce repeated work while preserving user control.
4. Show the useful numbers plainly. Calories, macros, portions, dates, and meal labels should be scannable without forcing the user into hidden detail screens.
5. Design for the current task. Each screen should prioritize the user's immediate job: log, confirm, correct, review, or adjust settings.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA as the baseline for contrast, readable text, semantic labels, focus states, and error messaging.

Mobile controls should use large tap targets and predictable platform affordances. Motion should be purposeful and respect reduced-motion preferences. Voice logging must supplement typed and tapped workflows, not replace them; every core action must remain possible without speech input.

The product should support metric-first food logging and avoid assumptions that all foods, brands, or portions are US-centric.
